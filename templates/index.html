Solar X-Ray Fluorescence Spectroscopy

When the Sun emits X-rays, they strike the lunar regolith and excite valence electrons in surface minerals. The excited atoms re-emit characteristic fluorescence photons at discrete energies unique to each element — a spectral fingerprint.

🌋 Equatorial Mare Basalts (|lat| < 30°)

Elevated Fe and Mg concentrations indicate ancient volcanic flood plains (e.g., Mare Imbrium, Mare Tranquillitatis). These regions crystallised from iron-rich magma oceans ~3.8 Gyr ago.

🏔 Polar Highlands (|lat| > 45°)

High Al and Si signatures denote the ancient anorthosite crust — primary crustal material formed when lighter minerals floated to the top of the cooling magma ocean.

📡 Reading the Charts

  • Spatial map: Marker size = CPS (photon rate); colour = elemental %. Clusters near the equator with large, red dots = active Mare volcanic plains.
  • Flux-CPS chart: Positive linear correlation confirms the physics — higher solar irradiance excites more fluorescence photons.
  • 3D Globe: Each point is projected to spherical coordinates (R=1). Rotate to see orbital ground-tracks wrapping the lunar geoid.
